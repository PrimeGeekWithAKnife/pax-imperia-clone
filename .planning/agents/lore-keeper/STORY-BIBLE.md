# Ex Nihilo — Story Bible

**Status:** Draft v2 — Based on Director Interviews, 22 March 2026

---

## Core Premise

The universe is vast beyond comprehension. Billions of galaxies, each containing billions of stars. Life is not rare — it is inevitable. Tens of thousands of species *could* exist. A few hundred minor species do exist in various primitive states across this single galaxy. But only a handful of major races have independently achieved space travel, through wildly different evolutionary processes, roughly at the same time.

There is no single event that caused first contact. No barrier fell. No signal went out. It is simply the statistical convergence of civilisations reaching the stars at overlapping moments in cosmic time. Like ships launched from different continents, they were always going to meet. The question was never *if*, but *when* — and *how*.

## The Name

"Ex Nihilo" — from nothing.

Whether by divine creation or the inevitable physics of gravity sparking a Big Bang, every species emerged from seemingly nothing. Humble microorganisms. Simple biological creatures. Beings assembled by long-forgotten masters. A hive mind that became aware when millions of smaller conscious beings connected. All rose from nothing, and all now face the same question: *what do we become?*

The name is not ironic. It is literal and philosophical. You begin with nothing. What you build — or destroy — is the only meaning the universe will ever have.

## Tone

**There is no single tone. Each species carries its own.**

- Some are gritty, dark, hostile, suspicious of others
- Idealists are Star Trek: "We're better together, let's share resources and learn"
- Some are Babylon 5 Vorlons and Shadows — ancient-feeling, philosophical, operating on a different level
- Some are desperate survivors fleeing dying worlds
- Some are eager conquerors who always dreamed of the stars

The game's tone shifts with the player's choices and species. A Khazari campaign should feel fundamentally different from a Sylvani one.

## Motivations for Expansion

Not all species expand for the same reason:

- **Ambition** — Some have always wanted to conquer the stars. It is their cultural destiny.
- **Curiosity** — Some expand because they must *know*. The universe is a question.
- **Survival** — One or two must leave their planet because it is dying or running out of resources.
- **Cataclysm** — Some face a great extinction. Expansion is not a choice but a last resort.
- **Inevitability** — Some simply grew until they had nowhere left to go but up.

Each species should have a clear, specific reason for being in space. This is the emotional core of their story.

## Major Species

Currently 15 major spacefaring species exist, each with unique traits, racial buildings, and internal conflicts. The roster covers a wide range of narrative niches: energy beings (Luminari), symbiotic parasites (Vethara), ancient machine relics (Kaelenth), nanoscale collectives (Thyriaq), dimensional explorers (Aethyn), religious zealots with Devourer-foreshadowing (Orivani), and elemental silicon forgers (Pyrenth).

## Pre-Game Awareness

First contact is NOT simultaneous for everyone:

- Some species are already **aware of others through remote observation** — telescopes, signal interception, long-range scanning. They know something is out there but have never met in person.
- At least one pair has **established communications** — they've been talking but have never physically met. The gap between "we've exchanged messages" and "we've shaken hands" is enormous.
- **Language research is race-specific.** You don't research "alien languages" generically. You research Khazari, or Sylvani, or Drakmari. Each is a separate endeavour. This creates natural diplomatic bottlenecks — you might be able to talk to two species but not the third.
- Most species begin truly unaware of the others.

## Natural Racial Biases

Species have **natural compatibility biases** based on biology, temperament, and values. These are starting conditions, not destiny:

- Biases can shift over time through diplomacy, trade, shared experience, or betrayal
- Some pairings are naturally complementary (e.g., a trading species and a manufacturing species)
- Some are naturally antagonistic (e.g., Sylvani organic-growth philosophy vs. Nexari digital-assimilation)
- Environmental incompatibility creates tension (species that need the same planets compete; species that need different ones don't)

## Faith & Religion

Faith is complex and varies enormously by species:

- **Some species are strictly religious** with organised faiths, scriptures, clergy, and dogma
- **Some have no religion at all** — the concept is alien to them
- **Some have guiding spiritual beliefs** without organised religion — no "bible," but a shared sense of something greater
- **Species need individual faith systems developed** as part of their codex

### Population Faith Segments

Religion is not binary. Populations have a distribution:

- **Fanatics** — Devout believers who will die for their faith. Dangerous if directed. Powerful if channelled.
- **Observant** — Regular worshippers. They attend, they believe, they follow the rules.
- **Casual** — Culturally religious. They observe holidays, follow some customs, don't think too hard about it.
- **Indifferent** — Registered as faith-leaning but don't really care. Go with the flow.
- **Secular** — No faith. May be actively hostile to religion.

### Faith as a Political Tool

- Rulers may **encourage a specific faith** that doesn't exist yet — manufacture belief for control
- Rulers may **suppress an existing faith** — creating underground movements and martyrs
- **Religious figures may hold political office** or wield serious influence over the masses
- **Regional variation** — different planets/regions within an empire may have different beliefs, creating internal tension
- Faith affects happiness, stability, and susceptibility to propaganda

## Minor Species

A few hundred minor species exist across the galaxy in various primitive states.

### Discovery & Nature

- Minor species appear **on planets** — pre-spaceflight civilisations the player can discover
- They are limited to **one star system, perhaps one planet, or even a single continent**
- They have unique properties, cultures, and potential

### Interaction Options

When discovering a minor species, the player can:

- **Observe** — Prime directive. Study them. Learn from their biology or culture.
- **Integrate** — Invite them into your empire. They become citizens with their own needs and opinions.
- **Uplift** — Teach them technology. Accelerate their development.
- **Exploit** — Cheap labour, experimental subjects, resource extraction.
- **Enslave** — Forced labour. Efficient but morally devastating.
- **Ignore** — Leave them alone. Until they give you a reason not to.
- **Research** — Study their unique biology or technology for scientific benefit.

### Minor Species Evolution

- If left alone for a long time, minor species have a **low chance of evolving into a spacefaring civilisation** — a new major power emerging mid-game
- They might have **unique properties** that make them great allies or trade partners
- This creates a strategic tension: exploit them now or invest in them for future benefit?

### The Trojan Horse

**Post-integration revolt is a real threat.**

A minor species that has been integrated into your empire may be biding their time. They've learned your technology, adapted to your systems, studied your weaknesses. And now they want to take over.

Perhaps it's driven by:
- Religious differences — their faith is incompatible with your governance
- Ideological grounds — they believe in freedom, you run a dictatorship
- It was their plan all along — a Trojan horse strategy. Appear weak, get absorbed, learn, adapt, strike

This makes the "integrate" option genuinely risky in a way that creates compelling gameplay.

## The Grand Design

There is no grand design — except the one you make.

Like discovering a new continent in the 16th century, the age of galactic expansion brings:
- Great upheaval
- Learning and discovery
- Disease and disaster
- War and friendship
- Alliances and betrayals
- The slow, painful process of becoming better

Some species may want to become the next creators. Others may wish to join in galactic harmony. The mission is subjective. The player decides what "victory" means.

## Ethical Audit Trail

**The game watches how you play.**

Every major decision is recorded and scored along ethical dimensions:

- **Ruthless ↔ Merciful** — How do you treat the conquered? The weak?
- **Honest ↔ Deceptive** — Do you honour treaties? Do you double-cross allies?
- **Just ↔ Corrupt** — Is your governance fair? Do you exploit your own people?
- **Diplomatic ↔ Aggressive** — Do you talk first or shoot first?
- **Ecological ↔ Exploitative** — Do you preserve worlds or strip-mine them?

This audit trail affects:
- How other species perceive you (reputation)
- The tone of victory/defeat epilogues
- Whether species rally to your banner when the Devourer arrives
- Loading screen commentary and narrative flavour

A genocidal conqueror gets a very different post-victory experience than a benevolent unifier.

## The Post-Victory Reveal: The Devourer

**THIS IS THE GAME'S GREATEST SECRET.**

Once the player achieves "victory" — ANY victory condition — the story springs its trap. The Devourer doesn't care how you won. It has a job to do.

### What They Are

The Devourers are a **biologically engineered race**, created by a master race in a galaxy so distant and remote the player will never interact with their creators. They are:

- **Fully sentient** — brilliant thinkers, capable of strategy, adaptation, creativity
- **Programmed** — despite their intelligence, they have a directive they cannot override
- **Not hostile per se** — they have no animosity toward the galaxy's inhabitants
- **Harvesters** — their job is to collect all energy from stars, harvest minerals from planets, and salvage any useful technology they find

### Their Goal

The Devourer force is an **expeditionary harvesting fleet**. Their mission:

- Extract energy from every star
- Harvest valuable minerals from every planet
- Collect any technology worth preserving
- If species step aside, the Devourers will simply do their work and leave — a dark, hollow galaxy with emptied planets and dimmed stars behind them
- If species resist, they are eliminated. Not out of hatred. Out of efficiency.

**At a good clip, they can harvest 80% of a galaxy's useful resources in a decade.**

### The Military Reality

- The Devourer's technology is roughly **10:1 superior** to the player's best
- One Devourer capital ship can comfortably defeat 10 of the player's equivalent vessels
- The force the player encounters is **expeditionary** — not their full civilisation
- Through research, massive fleets, superior weapons, good tactics, and pooling ALL available resources, the player can make the Devourers retreat **if they lose more than two-thirds of their expeditionary force**
- **But they will be back.** (In the next game/expansion.)

### The Player's Role

As the "victor" of the galaxy, the player is now its **major force**. All remaining species look to them for leadership and salvation — whether the player earned that trust or seized it by force.

The ethical audit trail matters enormously here:
- An empire that made allies has allies to call upon
- An empire that made enemies faces the Devourer alone
- An empire that uplifted minor species may find unexpected reinforcements
- An empire that exploited everyone may find rebellion at the worst possible moment

### Foreshadowing During Normal Gameplay

The player should be able to **investigate clues** during normal gameplay:

- Anomaly events at the galaxy edge
- Research projects to study strange signals
- Expeditions to sealed wormholes
- Decoding precursor warnings
- Vaelori psychic visions
- Sylvani ecological data showing the Withering pattern

These should give the observant player an inkling: *something is coming. I should build defences.* But never enough to spoil the reveal.

## Conquest and Occupation

Conquest is not one thing. It depends entirely on who is conquering:

- **Genocidal** — Raze everything, kill everyone. Some species are capable of this.
- **Subjugation** — The conquered live, but as subjects. Their culture is suppressed.
- **Integration** — The conquered are absorbed into the empire. They keep some identity.
- **Assimilation** — Borg-like. The conquered become the conqueror (Nexari).
- **Profiteering** — The conquered are exploited for resources. Slavery, forced labour.
- **Benevolent rule** — Rare, but possible. "We conquered you to show you a better way."

The game should mechanically support different occupation policies. Diplomatic consequences, happiness effects, and long-term stability should vary.

## The Ascension Project

The research victory is not about becoming gods. It is about **immortality**.

Transcending physical and mental frailty, ailment, and biological breakdown. No more disease. No more aging. No more death. The species that achieves this has solved the fundamental problem of existence.

Some may *think* of themselves as god-like. If you look upon an ape, would you not feel superior? But the reality is more nuanced — they have merely taken the next step. They are not gods. They are what comes after mortality.

**This makes the Devourer reveal even more devastating.** The species that just achieved immortality now faces the one thing that can still destroy them.

## What Lies Beyond

More galaxies. More species. The great unknown.

The galaxy the player explores is one of billions. There is always more out there — some of it wondrous, some of it terrifying. The Devourer came from out there. What else might?
