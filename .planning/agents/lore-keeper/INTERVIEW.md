# Ex Nihilo -- Creative Interview & Lore Audit

**Prepared by:** Lore Keeper
**For:** The Director
**Date:** 22 March 2026

> *"Ex Nihilo" -- from nothing. Eight civilisations crawling out of the dark,
> blinking into the light of stars they never knew they shared. The name
> itself is the thesis: you begin with nothing, and what you build -- or
> destroy -- is the story.*

---

## Section 1: What We Have

### 1.1 The Species -- An Audit

Eight prebuilt species exist, each with a distinctive biological concept, stat profile, and environmental niche. Here is what the lore already tells us, and what it does not.

| Species | Concept | Strengths | Personality | Home Environment |
|---------|---------|-----------|-------------|------------------|
| **Vaelori** | Crystalline psionic beings | Research 9, Espionage 8 | Contemplative, alien, collective | Cold, low-gravity, methane/nitrogen |
| **Khazari** | Volcanic reptilian industrialists | Construction 9, Combat 9 | Blunt, material, warlike | Extreme heat, high-gravity, CO2/SO2 |
| **Sylvani** | Botanical hive intelligence | Reproduction 8, Research 8 | Patient, optimal, slow-burning | Temperate, earth-like, O2/N2 |
| **Nexari** | Cybernetic collective (bio+AI) | Research 9, Construction 8 | Relentless, logical, disturbing | Highly adaptable to almost anything |
| **Drakmari** | Deep-ocean apex predators | Combat 9, Reproduction 7 | Aggressive, cunning, instinctual | Warm, high-gravity, ocean worlds |
| **Teranos** | Primate generalists (human analogue) | Economy 7, Diplomacy 7 | Adaptable, connected, mercantile | Earth-standard |
| **Zorvathi** | Insectoid hive tunnellers | Construction 9, Reproduction 8 | Efficient, relentless, silent | Cold, high-gravity, atmospheric omnivore |
| **Ashkari** | Nomadic scavenger-engineers | Espionage 9, Economy 8 | Cunning, rootless, survivors | Extremely adaptable (lost homeworld) |

**What the species lore already does well:**
- Every species has a clear biological hook tied to gameplay stats (Khazari evolved in volcanic highlands, ergo construction and combat)
- Environmental preferences create genuine colonisation tension -- Vaelori and Khazari can never comfortably share a world
- Special abilities (psychic, silicon_based, photosynthetic, cybernetic, hive_mind, aquatic, nomadic, subterranean) hint at deep mechanical differentiation
- The Ashkari have the only real *narrative* -- they lost their homeworld 3,000 years ago. This is the most compelling backstory in the set

**What is missing:**
- **Culture and values.** We know *what* each species is biologically, but not *who* they are. What do the Khazari believe? What do the Sylvani fear? What art does a Vaelori crystal make? What do the Drakmari worship, if anything?
- **Internal conflict.** Every species reads as monolithic. Are there Khazari pacifists? Nexari who refused integration? Sylvani pruned-off branches that went feral?
- **Relationships.** No species description references any other species. Have they met before? Do any share ancient history? Is there an established enmity or alliance?
- **Motivation for expansion.** Only the Ashkari have a reason to be in space (they lost their home). Why are the others expanding *now*? What changed?
- **Names, language, aesthetic.** No naming conventions, no sense of what their ships, cities, or leaders are called. The Khazari should *sound* different from the Sylvani.
- **Scale of civilisation.** How old are these species as spacefaring races? Are they ancient and stagnant, or young and hungry?

**Contradictions and tensions:**
- The Nexari are described as "cybernetic" and "hive_mind" but their backstory says they were once a "fragile biological species driven by intense curiosity and a terror of death." This is fascinating -- but is it tragic or triumphant? The lore is neutral where it should take a position.
- The Zorvathi are also "hive_mind" and the Nexari are also "hive_mind" -- but these are fundamentally different kinds of collective consciousness (biological swarm vs. digital network). The game mechanic treats them identically. The lore should not.
- The Teranos are clearly humans with the serial numbers filed off ("warm-blooded primate species"). This is fine, but the game should either commit to them being us, or make them alien enough that the resemblance is unsettling rather than transparent.
- "Faith" is a game resource produced by Entertainment Complexes and Diplomatic Quarters, but no species lore mentions religion, spirituality, or cultural belief systems. The Theocracy government exists. What are they theocratic *about*?

### 1.2 The Technology Tree -- A Story in 81 Steps

The tech tree progresses through five ages:

1. **Nano-Atomic** -- Foundations: quantum theory, molecular biology, nanotechnology, basic weapons and drives
2. **Fusion** -- Power and reach: plasma physics, graviton theory, xenobiology, wormhole stabilisation, cruiser hulls
3. **Nano-Fusion** -- Intelligence and transcendence begins: AI, teleportation, phase shields, precursor archaeology, terraforming
4. **Anti-Matter** -- Mastery: dark matter/energy, consciousness studies, mind control, battleship yards, Dyson collectors
5. **Singularity** -- Apotheosis: reality manipulation, temporal shields, world engines, genetic apotheosis, the Ascension Project

**What the tech tree tells us narratively:**
- The universe has a clear arc from material mastery to existential transcendence. The final technology -- "Ascension Project" -- describes a civilisation that "transcends the game entirely." This is a *research victory condition*, and it is essentially apotheosis: becoming a god.
- There are **precursors**. "Precursor Archaeology" (nano-fusion age) explicitly states: "artefacts left by civilisations that transcended the physical plane millions of years ago." This is a massive lore implication that is never explored elsewhere.
- **Consciousness** is a major theme. The tree moves from Neural Enhancement to Consciousness Studies to Transcendence Theory to Genetic Apotheosis to Species Evolution. The implication is that scientific progress leads inevitably toward the dissolution of the boundary between mind and universe.
- **Wormholes are natural phenomena** that the player learns to stabilise. "Naturally-occurring micro-wormholes" suggests the galaxy's wormhole network is not artificial -- or is it? If precursors existed, did they build the wormholes?
- **Mind Control** exists as a researchable technology. This has enormous ethical implications that the game currently does not address.
- **Psionic Subjugation** -- "Populations can be pacified, loyalties rewritten, and enemy worlds turned without a shot being fired." This is, functionally, the erasure of free will on a planetary scale. The lore presents it without moral commentary.

**What is missing from the tech tree:**
- No tech descriptions reference specific species -- they are all written as if a generic civilisation is discovering them. But a Vaelori researching "Psionic Sensitivity" would have a fundamentally different experience from a Khazari doing the same.
- No technologies address diplomacy, governance, or social science. All progress is STEM-focused. Where is political philosophy? Economics? Sociology? Art?
- No technologies have failure states, risks, or unintended consequences mentioned in their descriptions. Every discovery is presented as progress. But what about the AI that rebels? The terraforming that kills a native ecology? The psionic amplifier that drives its users mad?

### 1.3 Government Types -- Political Philosophy in 14 Flavours

The 14 government types range from Democracy to Forced Labour to Hive Mind. The descriptions are functional and well-written, but they are entirely *mechanical*: what bonuses and penalties apply. None describe what it *feels like* to live under that government, what historical precedent inspired it, or what philosophical position it represents.

**Notable observations:**
- **Forced Labour** exists alongside **Equality** and **Democracy**. The game allows the player to run a slave state. What are the narrative consequences?
- **Hive Mind** states that "the concept of happiness is meaningless." This is a profound philosophical claim -- that individuality is prerequisite to joy. Is this the game's position?
- **Theocracy** generates a "faith bonus" -- but faith in *what*? No religion or spiritual system is described for any species.
- **Technocracy** has "population grows slowly" as a penalty -- why? Is the implication that rational societies are emotionally unfulfilling? That science-led governance suppresses the human (or alien) drive to reproduce?
- There is no government type for **anarchism**, **post-scarcity communism**, or **AI governance** (despite AI Governor being a technology). These are notable absences for a far-future setting.

### 1.4 Buildings -- Infrastructure With No Soul

The 33 buildings are mechanically solid but narratively thin. Racial buildings are the exception -- the Khazari War Forge, the Ashkari Black Market, the Nexari Assimilation Node -- these tell stories. But universal buildings like "Factory" and "Power Plant" are purely functional.

**Standout narrative buildings:**
- **Assimilation Node** (Nexari) -- "steadily converts alien population units into Nexari-substrate citizens." This is cultural genocide dressed as efficiency. The lore does not flinch, but neither does it comment.
- **Predator Arena** (Drakmari) -- "ritual combat against live prey." What is the live prey? Prisoners? Animals? Other Drakmari?
- **Black Market** (Ashkari) -- Legitimises piracy and smuggling as a state function. Fascinating for a displaced people.
- **Crystal Resonance Chamber** (Vaelori) -- "synchronising the minds of every researcher on the planet." Beautiful and alien.

## Section 2: Thematic Questions for the Director

### The Universe

1. **Why now?** The galaxy has eight spacefaring civilisations -- but why are they encountering each other *now*, at the start of the game? Did something change? A barrier fell? A signal was detected? Or is first contact simply the statistical inevitability of expansion?

2. **The wormhole question.** Wormholes connect star systems in the game. The tech tree calls them "naturally-occurring micro-wormholes." Are they truly natural? Or are they the remnants of a precursor network -- ancient infrastructure left behind by civilisations that ascended? If so, did the wormholes *activate* recently, and is that why everyone is suddenly meeting?

3. **Precursors.** The tech tree explicitly references "civilisations that transcended the physical plane millions of years ago." How much do we want to lean into this? Are precursor ruins scattered across the map? Do they whisper? Are they dangerous? Are the precursors *gone*, or merely *elsewhere*?

4. **What is beyond the map edge?** Is the galaxy finite and known, or is there something out there -- a darkness, a wall, a signal, a threat? Does the edge of the map represent the edge of explored space, or the edge of something more fundamental?

5. **How old is civilisation?** Are these young species taking their first steps, or ancient ones finally colliding? The Ashkari have been spacefaring for at least 3,000 years. Are the others comparable, or is there a huge disparity in age?

### Tone and Inspiration

6. **Where on the hope-to-horror spectrum does Ex Nihilo sit?** Star Trek's optimism says "we can be better." The Expanse's realism says "we will be ourselves, but louder." Lovecraft's cosmicism says "we don't matter." 2001 says "something vast is watching." Where are we?

7. **Is this a game about building a better galaxy, or surviving a hostile one?** Can the player make utopia? Or is every victory built on someone else's ruin?

8. **What is the emotional target for the player?** Should they feel *powerful* (Stellaris), *clever* (Civilization), *awestruck* (Outer Wilds), *paranoid* (XCOM), or *conflicted* (Spec Ops: The Line)?

9. **How much humour is allowed?** Is the tone relentlessly serious, or are there moments of absurdity, personality, or warmth? Can a loading screen tip make the player smile?

### Ethics and Philosophy

10. **Can the player commit atrocities, and does the game judge them for it?** Mind Control, Forced Labour, Assimilation Nodes, orbital bombardment -- these are tools of oppression and destruction. Does the game present them neutrally as strategic options? Or are there consequences -- diplomatic penalties, internal unrest, event chains that ask "was it worth it?"

11. **What does "Forced Labour" mean?** Is this chattel slavery? Indentured servitude? Penal labour? The government type exists mechanically, but its moral weight is undefined. Some players will choose it for the construction bonus. Should the game make them *feel* that choice?

12. **Is conquest genocide?** When one species conquers another's homeworld, what happens to the population? Are they enslaved? Assimilated? Exterminated? Left alone but taxed? This single question defines the moral universe of the game.

13. **The Nexari Assimilation Node.** Converting alien populations into Nexari-substrate citizens is presented as a building. Is this a horror, a mercy, or simply a fact? Does the assimilated population retain memories? Do they consider themselves saved or destroyed?

### Species Identity

14. **What makes each species *feel* different beyond numbers?** Can we give each species a distinct musical motif, colour palette, architectural style, naming convention, and narrative voice? When a player chooses the Vaelori, should the entire UI feel crystalline and ethereal? When they play the Khazari, should it feel volcanic and brutal?

15. **What do these species value?** The Vaelori value knowledge -- but is it knowledge for its own sake, or for power? The Khazari value strength -- but is it the strength of the forge or the fist? The Sylvani value patience -- but do they value individual lives, or only the whole? Every species needs a philosophical position, not just a biological one.

16. **Are there dissidents within each species?** Are there Nexari who unplugged? Zorvathi drones who achieved individual consciousness? Khazari poets who were shamed for their softness? Internal conflict makes species feel alive.

### The Endgame

17. **What does the Ascension Project mean?** The tech tree's final technology says the civilisation "transcends the game entirely, reshaping the galaxy in its own image and ascending to a plane of existence beyond comprehension." Is this a good thing? Is the player's species *leaving* the universe? Becoming the new precursors? This is the most philosophically loaded moment in the game and it currently has one sentence of description.

18. **What does conquest victory mean?** If you conquer every star system, have you achieved peace or committed the greatest crime in galactic history? Does the game's epilogue reflect the *means* by which you won?

19. **Is there a "bad ending"?** Can the galaxy be left worse than you found it? Can the player accidentally (or deliberately) destroy something irreplaceable?

20. **Do victories overlap?** If one civilisation ascends, does it affect the others? Do they notice? Is there a galaxy-spanning event -- a pulse of light, a silence, a fundamental change in the laws of physics?

### Mystery and Discovery

21. **Are there ancient ruins?** Precursor Archaeology is a technology, but are there physical sites on planets -- shattered megastructures, buried archives, inexplicable machines? Can the player find something that changes everything they thought they knew?

22. **What is the most alien thing in this universe?** Every 4X game has a "wow" moment -- a discovery that makes the player sit back and stare. What is ours? A living star? A planet that thinks? A signal from outside the galaxy? A wormhole that leads to a place that should not exist?

## Section 4: Story Hooks

Five narrative frameworks that could unify the universe. These are seeds, not finished stories -- each would shape the game's tone, mystery, and endgame differently.

---

### Hook 1: The Silence

*For a million years, every civilisation in the galaxy was alone. Not because they were rare -- there were thousands -- but because something kept them apart. A field, a frequency, a wall in the quantum foam. Then, forty years before the game begins, the wall fell. Wormholes opened. Subspace cleared. Signals that had been absorbed for aeons suddenly arrived all at once.*

*No one knows why the Silence ended. The precursors, who built the wall, are long gone -- transcended or dead. Their ruins dot the galaxy like headstones. Some species believe the wall was protection. Others believe it was a cage. The truth may be worse than either.*

**What this gives us:** A reason for simultaneous first contact. A mystery to drive mid-game exploration. A source of dread -- was the wall keeping something out, or keeping us in?

---

### Hook 2: The Inheritance

*The precursors did not die. They ascended -- shed their physical forms and became something beyond matter, beyond time. But they left behind a test. The wormhole network, the scattered ruins, the technologies that can be excavated but never fully understood -- it is all a crucible. The first civilisation to reach the Ascension Project does not merely transcend. It inherits the galaxy. It becomes the new steward.*

*The precursors are watching. They have always been watching. And they are not patient.*

**What this gives us:** A coherent reason for precursor ruins. A narrative weight to the Ascension Project. A ticking clock -- if no civilisation proves worthy, the precursors may decide the experiment has failed.

---

### Hook 3: The Dark Forest

*The galaxy is not empty. It is quiet. And there is a difference.*

*The wormhole network was not built by the precursors. It was built by something else entirely -- something that uses the wormholes to listen. Every civilisation that becomes loud enough, advanced enough, powerful enough, attracts its attention. The precursors did not transcend. They were consumed.*

*The Ascension Project is not a victory. It is a signal flare.*

**What this gives us:** A late-game crisis. A reason why precursor ruins are silent and broken. An existential threat that reframes every player choice -- expand too fast and you attract the Listener. Stay quiet and you may never be strong enough to survive.

---

### Hook 4: The Garden

*The galaxy is alive. Not metaphorically -- literally. The wormhole network is a root system. The stars are nodes in a vast, slow, vegetable intelligence that thinks on timescales of billions of years. The precursors discovered this and tried to communicate. They succeeded. They were absorbed -- not destroyed, but folded into the galactic consciousness like thoughts into a dreaming mind.*

*Every species in the game is, from the galaxy's perspective, a microorganism on its skin. The question is not whether the galaxy notices -- it will, eventually. The question is what it does when it wakes up.*

**What this gives us:** A Solaris-style cosmic mystery. A profound recontextualisation of exploration. A late-game event where the galaxy itself becomes a character. Particular resonance with the Sylvani, whose biological-network intelligence mirrors the galaxy's own.

---

### Hook 5: Ex Nihilo (the literal reading)

*There is no precursor mystery. There is no cosmic threat. There is no grand design.*

*The universe is exactly what it appears to be: vast, indifferent, and beautiful. Eight civilisations evolved independently on worlds separated by impossible distances, and through ingenuity, desperation, or accident, they learned to cross those distances. They have nothing in common except the shared condition of being alive and alone in a universe that does not care.*

*The story is not about what came before. It is about what comes next. What these species build, together or against each other, is the only meaning the universe will ever have. The game is called "From Nothing" because that is the truth: there is nothing here except what you make.*

**What this gives us:** Existential weight without mysticism. A tone that respects the player's agency -- *you* are the story, not some precursor puzzle box. A philosophical position (absurdist, humanist) that makes the Ascension Project feel earned rather than scripted. The loneliness of being the only meaning in an empty cosmos.

## What Happens Next

This document is the beginning of a conversation, not the end of one. Once the Director has responded to these questions -- even partially, even with "I don't know yet" -- the Lore Keeper can begin:

- Writing a **Story Bible** that codifies the answers into canonical lore
- Drafting **species culture documents** with naming conventions, values, aesthetics, and internal conflicts
- Revising **tech tree descriptions** to reflect the chosen narrative framework
- Writing **event text** for discoveries, crises, and diplomatic encounters
- Creating **loading screen lore fragments** that build the universe one sentence at a time

The universe is waiting. Let's build it from nothing.
